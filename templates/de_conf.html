Prevalence of Dental Caries and Patterns of Dental Defects among Preschool Children in Erbil, Kurdistan Region, Iraq

Dr. Ghalib Rahman Hawrami - M.Sc. Prosthodontic (Lecturer)

Dr. Halat Farhad Younis - B.D.S. General Dental Practitioner

Erbil Polytechnic University, Erbil, Iraq

Dental Assistant Department

Background & Significance

  • Early Childhood Caries (ECC) - A significant global public health concern
  • Most prevalent chronic oral disease in young children
  • Affects millions of children worldwide
  • Causes pain, discomfort, and impacts overall well-being
  • Limited comprehensive data available for Erbil region
ECC: Presence of one or more decayed, missing, or filled tooth surfaces in any primary tooth in children under 6 years

Problem Statement

  • Notable research gap in comprehensive epidemiological data for Iraq, particularly Erbil Governorate
  • Previous studies limited in scope or focused on older age groups
  • Lack of data hampers development of targeted interventions
  • Evidence-based policies needed to address ECC in this population
Critical knowledge gap regarding oral health status of preschool-aged children in Erbil

Study Objectives

Primary Objectives:

  • Determine the prevalence of dental caries and dental defects among 4-5 year old children
  • Assess severity of dental caries using DMFI indices

Secondary Objectives:

  • Investigate associations between dental defects and demographic factors
  • Provide comprehensive baseline data for targeted interventions
  • Inform oral health policies in Erbil Governorate

Methodology

354
Children Examined
4-5
Age Range (Years)
  • Study Design: Cross-sectional census study
  • Location: Faxir Mergasuri Preschool, Erbil
  • Duration: December 2023 - April 2024
  • Assessment Tool: DMFI Indices (Decay, Missing, Filled, Indicated for extraction)
  • Examiners: Two calibrated dentists using ICDAS

DMFI Index Components

D
Decay - Visually detected untreated caries
M
Missing - Teeth absent due to caries
F
Filled - Restored tooth surfaces
I
Indicated for Extraction - Severe caries

Global Comparison

Our Study vs. International Data:

  • Erbil (Our Study): 69.75% prevalence
  • Nigeria (Oyedele et al.): 52.5% prevalence
  • Saudi Arabia (Al-Jabir et al.): 83% prevalence
  • Previous Erbil Study: 68.3% prevalence
Our findings align with global trends showing high ECC prevalence in developing regions

Contributing Risk Factors

Biological Factors:

  • Cariogenic bacteria (Streptococcus mutans)
  • Fermentable carbohydrates interaction

Behavioral Factors:

  • Frequent sugary beverage/snack consumption
  • Poor oral hygiene practices
  • Inadequate fluoride exposure

Social Factors:

  • Socioeconomic status
  • Limited access to dental care
  • Parental education level

Clinical Implications

Immediate Concerns:

  • High unmet treatment needs
  • Rapid progression of dental problems
  • Early tooth loss complications

Long-term Impact:

  • Increased risk of caries in permanent teeth
  • Potential orthodontic complications
  • Impact on quality of life and development
  • Economic burden on families and healthcare system

Study Limitations

  • Single-site design: Limited generalizability to other populations
  • Visual examination only: No radiographic assessment
  • Cross-sectional nature: Cannot establish causality
  • Limited socioeconomic data: Missing important confounding factors
Despite limitations, provides valuable baseline data for Erbil region

Prevention Recommendations

Primary Prevention:

  • Community water fluoridation programs
  • Oral health education in preschools
  • Parental education programs
  • Dietary counseling and guidance

Secondary Prevention:

  • Regular dental screening programs
  • Fluoride varnish applications
  • Early detection and intervention protocols

Treatment Recommendations

Immediate Actions:

  • Establish school-based dental clinics
  • Training programs for dental professionals
  • Mobile dental units for underserved areas
  • Referral systems for complex cases

Long-term Strategy:

  • Integration with primary healthcare
  • Policy development for oral health
  • Insurance coverage for pediatric dentistry

Future Research Directions

  • Multi-site studies across various socioeconomic areas
  • Longitudinal studies to track progression over time
  • Advanced diagnostics including radiographic assessments
  • Socioeconomic analysis of contributing factors
  • Intervention effectiveness through RCTs
  • Parental knowledge and attitudes assessment
  • Cost-effectiveness studies of prevention programs

Conclusions

  • High prevalence of dental defects (69.75%) among preschoolers in Erbil
  • Decay is the most common problem affecting 86.16% of children
  • Significant unmet treatment needs identified
  • Age-related progression of dental problems observed
  • Urgent need for comprehensive oral health programs
  • Early intervention can prevent progression to severe cases
Call to Action: Implement targeted oral health promotion, preventive interventions, and improved access to early dental care
